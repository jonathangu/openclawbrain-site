Post 3

Evaluation: prove it without pretending

March 2026 | v12.2.6+ series

The evidence should do the talking, not the marketing copy. This page leads with the strongest published benchmarks, then walks through what each level of evidence proves, where the claims stop, and why a guide that follows knowledge relationships outperforms keyword search on these workloads.

Proven now

Recorded sessions: the learned guide answers 97.5% correctly vs. 89.6% for the best search method — roughly 1 in 13 queries where search fails, the guide gets right. Sparse-feedback (10 seeds): 92.0% vs. 67.1% accuracy, about 5x less context per query, and the guide wins 9 of 10 seeds head-to-head.

Needs real-traffic tests

Shadow traffic (mirroring real queries in the background) and narrow live rollout evidence are needed before claiming better outcomes on live OpenClaw workloads.

Not claimed

No blanket performance wins, no production latency or cost claims, and no claim that controlled tests alone prove production superiority.

These are benchmark results, not live rollout evidence. They sit in the middle of the evidence ladder: stronger than the small controlled test, but still below real-traffic proof.

The small controlled test: can the guide find the right facts?

This is intentionally narrow: 4 fixed queries testing whether the guide surfaces exactly the right pieces of knowledge. Think of it as asking four specific questions and checking whether the librarian pulled the right books. Keyword search finds none. The learned guide finds all of them.

MethodRight answersWhat this tests
Keyword search 0/4 The floor: searching by word similarity with no knowledge of how facts relate.
Link-following 1/4 Walking obvious connections between facts, without learning which connections matter.
Map structure only 2/4 Using the knowledge map's built-in relationships, before any learning. The "useful from day one" baseline.
Learned guide 4/4 The same map after background learning has improved which paths the guide follows.

The learning curve matters as much as the final score. Map structure alone stays flat at 2/4 across all 16 rounds, while the learned guide reaches 4/4 by round 14 — like a librarian who gets better each week at knowing which shelf solves which question. What this proves — and where it stops

The controlled test plus the two benchmarks prove something real but bounded: the knowledge map helps retrieval from day one, background learning makes it better, and the learned guide beats credible search methods on frozen workloads. Why? Not magic — the guide follows relationships between facts instead of keyword similarity, it learns from corrections (search has no feedback loop), and it sends less context for better results. It still does not prove better outcomes on live OpenClaw traffic — that requires real-traffic testing and live rollout evidence.

The evidence ladder: what each level proves

LevelWhat it provesWhat you need to see
Level 1: Controlled test The guide and learning machinery work correctly on a small, fully reproducible set. The "does it even work?" check. Exact commands, commit SHA, summary tables, learning curves, and per-query breakdowns.
Level 2: Recorded-session eval The learned guide beats real alternatives on larger, frozen workloads. The "does it win a fair comparison?" check. Published now. The two benchmark bundles with query fixtures, scoring tables, and per-query outputs.
Level 3: Shadow traffic The guide helps on real OpenClaw traffic mirrored in the background. The "does it work on real queries?" check. Not yet published. Mirrored traffic, side-by-side metrics, and disagreement traces.
Level 4: Narrow live rollout The guide serves real users and measurably improves outcomes. The "does it help in production?" check. Not yet published. Success rate, correction rate, prompt size, latency, cost.

The reporting contract

  1. Every nontrivial claim needs a command, an artifact path, and a commit SHA.
  2. If an artifact doesn't exist yet, the table cell stays TBD.
  3. Don't describe the 4-query controlled test as a product win on OpenClaw traffic.
