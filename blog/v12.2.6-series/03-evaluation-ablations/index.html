Post 3

Evaluation: prove it without pretending

March 2026 | v12.2.6+ series

OpenClawBrain needs a cleaner evidence story than "the simulations look good." This page starts with the strongest published benchmarks, then walks through the evidence ladder — what each rung actually proves, and where the claims stop — so the proof scope is obvious on first read.

Proven now

Recorded sessions: the learned router answers 97.5% of queries correctly vs. 89.6% for the best RAG baseline — roughly 1 in 13 queries where RAG fails, the learned router gets right. Sparse-feedback (10 seeds): 92.0% vs. 67.1% accuracy, about 5x less context per query, and the learned router wins 9 of 10 seeds head-to-head.

Needs production comparisons

Shadow traffic (mirroring real queries) and narrow online rollout artifacts are needed before claiming better outcomes on live OpenClaw workloads.

Not claimed

No blanket benchmark wins, no production latency or cost win, and no claim that deterministic build artifacts alone prove production superiority.

These are benchmark artifacts, not live product rollout evidence. They sit in the middle of the evidence ladder: stronger than the small deterministic mechanism test, but still below shadow-traffic and online rollout proof (which would show the router helping on real user queries).

Current workflow-proof slice

The current published slice is intentionally narrow: exact-target retrieval on 4 deterministic workflow queries. It is useful because it tests the core product claim directly: can the graph's built-in structural knowledge (the "graph prior") make OpenClawBrain useful before any learning has happened, and does background learning later improve the served routing function?

ModeExact-target successWhat this isolates
vector_topk 0/4 Semantic retrieval floor with no workflow structure or learned routing.
pointer_chase 1/4 Simple deterministic traversal over explicit links without a learned route policy.
graph_prior_only 2/4 Durable structure before learning; the "start useful fast" part of the story.
learned 4/4 The same memory layer after background learning has improved routing.

The learning curve matters as much as the endpoint. graph_prior_only stays flat at 2/4 across all 16 epochs, while learned first reaches 4/4 at epoch 14. The proof package now also points to per_query_matrix.csv and per_query_matrix.md, the 16-row scenario matrix showing which node IDs reached prompt context under each mode. Reproduce the figures from docs/reproduce-eval.md; compare the integration-level artifact bundle in docs/worked-example.md.

Mechanism proof versus product proof

The deterministic workflow slice plus the two benchmark bundles prove something real but bounded: the graph's built-in structure helps retrieval from day one, learned routing improves it further, and the learned router leads credible RAG baselines on frozen workloads. It still does not prove better outcomes on live OpenClaw traffic — that requires shadow-traffic and online rollout artifacts.

The evidence ladder (what each rung proves)

StageWhat it provesWhat artifacts are required
Rung 1: Deterministic workflow proof The routing and learning mechanics work correctly on a small, fully reproducible query set. This is the "does the machine even work?" test. Exact commands, commit SHA, summary.csv, learning_curve.csv, report.md, per_query_matrix.csv, and per_query_matrix.md.
Rung 2: Offline recorded-session eval The learned router beats real baselines on larger, frozen workloads. This is the "does it win a fair comparison?" test. Published now. recorded_h2h_relational_drift_001 and sparse_feedback_10seed with query fixtures, scoring tables, per-query outputs, and worked traces.
Rung 3: Shadow traffic The brain helps on real OpenClaw traffic (mirrored in the background, not yet serving users). This is the "does it work on real queries?" test. Not yet published. Mirrored traffic slice, side-by-side metrics, retained outputs, and disagreement traces.
Rung 4: Narrow online rollout The brain serves real users and measurably improves outcomes. This is the "does it help in production?" test. Not yet published. Success rate, correction rate, prompt size, latency, cost.

Reporting contract

  1. Every nontrivial claim needs a command, an artifact path, and a commit SHA.
  2. If an artifact does not exist yet, the table cell stays TBD.
  3. Do not summarize the 4-query deterministic workflow slice as a product win on OpenClaw traffic.
